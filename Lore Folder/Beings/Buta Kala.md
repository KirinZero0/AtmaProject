# Buta Kala

### Beings Lore | Lower Realm

---

## What They Are

Buta Kala are lower-realm spirit beings occupying a specific and understood role in Balinese cosmology — not evil, not benevolent, simply present and requiring management. The caru ceremonies exist because of them. The offerings at crossroads and shrines exist because of them. The entire liturgical practice of acknowledging the lower realm is, at its core, a negotiation with what the Buta Kala represent: forces that exist below the ordered world and will intrude on it if not properly attended to.

They are not malicious. They are hungry, and present, and operating according to their nature. Ritual practice keeps that nature contained.

---

## Appearance

Small — goblin-scale, with a crouching posture that makes them look smaller still. Dark skin, almost black. Wide mouth, fanged. Bulging eyes. Wild hair. The shape is recognizable from centuries of Balinese temple carvings — the Kala face above doorways, the demonic figures in the stonework corners, the creatures depicted receiving offerings at the base of shrines.

Balinese religious art has been depicting them accurately for a very long time. The ceremonies that contained them meant most people never saw them outside of stone.

---

## The Broken Ceremonies

Under the occupation, the ceremonies stopped. Rangda did not specifically target the caru rites — she did not need to. The occupation disrupted the conditions under which they could be performed: the priests were displaced, the sacred materials became unavailable or unaffordable, the community structures that organized collective ritual were broken apart by the same mechanisms that broke everything else.

The Buta Kala did not receive their offerings. The acknowledgment stopped. The negotiation ended without their agreement.

They crossed over.

---

## In The World

Buta Kala appear in the Middle Ground — near shrines, at crossroads, in offering sites where the ceremonies used to be performed. They are not guarding anything. They are not following orders. They are eating what is there because what is there is available and no one is stopping them.

The first encounter in most shrine areas: Natha approaches. A Buta Kala is crouched over the offering plate, eating. It looks up. If Natha moves toward the shrine it treats him as competition.

This is not a military confrontation. It is what happens when the ritual order fails and the things the ritual was managing notice.

---

## Destruction and Return

Buta Kala are not stranded the way spirit beasts are stranded. They did not cross over against their will and find themselves trapped. They came through because the boundary weakened and no one was performing the rites that maintained it. Destroying their material presence in the world sends them back to the lower realm — not liberation, not death, just return. They will be there when the ceremonies resume, waiting to be acknowledged again.

Whether the ceremonies will ever resume is a question tied to whether the veil is restored. Whether anyone will be left to perform them is a question tied to what happens to Devata.

---

## Notes

The Buta Kala are not enemies in a military sense. They have no allegiance to Rangda, no role in the occupation, no awareness of the larger conflict. They are simply what fills the space when the ritual practice that managed them collapses. Their presence in the Middle Ground is a symptom, not a cause.

The offering plates they were eating from are still at the shrines when Natha arrives. The flowers are scattered. The fruit is half-eaten. The stone face of the deity above the offering niche still has its expression.

These details are not interactive. They are just there.
