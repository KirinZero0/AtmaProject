# Devata Religion

## What It Was

Before Rangda's occupation, Devata had a living faith — temples, offerings, ceremonies, a cosmological understanding of the relationship between the material world and the divine. The theology was intact. The rituals had meaning behind them. The priests knew the correct order of things.

That was two hundred years ago.

## What Remains

Nobody alive remembers the original practice. The grandchildren of grandchildren of those who lived before the occupation have no living memory of what the faith looked like when it was whole. What survived is the surface — the forms without the content underneath.

The ceremonies still happen. Offerings are still made. Incense burns. Temples are maintained. Priests perform the gestures.

The gestures are in the wrong order. Nobody notices because nobody remembers the right order anymore.

The theology is gone — suppressed by Rangda, eroded by time, lost in the gap between generations. What replaced it is something that looks identical from a distance and is hollowed out up close. Devotion without understanding. Ritual without meaning. The shape of a faith that no longer exists performed sincerely by people who believe they are practicing it correctly.

## Barong Worship

The central figure of Devata's religion is Barong.

He was always significant — the Divine Lion, the protector, the force that sealed Rangda during the original incident. But in the 200 years since Dharma's failure and Rangda's takeover, Barong has become something closer to a messiah figure. The one who sealed her before. The one who will return. The protector the kingdom is still waiting for.

The population prays to Barong with complete sincerity. They do not know he is corrupted in the Spirit World — slowly poisoned across eight hundred years by the atma of the kings they also venerate. They do not know the mask exists, or what it costs, or that the protector they are praying for is already damaged by the prayers' predecessors.

Rangda did not suppress the Barong worship. She may have quietly encouraged it. A population praying for a savior that never arrives is easier to manage than one that has given up entirely. Hope without outlet keeps people still.

## The Statues

Across Devata — in temples, at shrine intersections, along ceremonial roads — stand statues of the past kings. The kings who wore the mask. The kings who sealed Rangda for eight hundred years and kept the cycle turning.

None of the statues show the kings' faces.

They show the Barong mask.

Two hundred years of occupation, and before that eight hundred years of the mask being the symbol of royal power and divine protection, have collapsed the kings and the mask into a single image. The people of Devata do not know what their kings looked like. They know what the mask looks like. To them, the kings ARE the mask — the face of protection, the face of the ones who kept them safe, the face of everything Rangda took away.

The statues hold weapons. Each one a different king's weapon, preserved in stone, surrounded by offerings left by people who venerate the king without knowing his name.

When Natha approaches these statues wearing the actual mask, the mask recognizes itself in stone. A memory surfaces. A dead king's combat knowledge bleeds through.

He is walking through a kingdom that worships the thing on his face. Every temple, every shrine, every masked stone figure — the same face looking back at him. He knows what the mask takes from him every time he dies.

Nobody in Devata knows either of those things fully. Not what they are worshipping. Not what it costs.
