# Wikananda

## The Last Royal Pedanda — Leader of the Rebellion

Relation: [[Prince Natha]], [[Sayan]]
## Overview

Wikananda is the leader of the rebellion against Rangda's two-hundred-year occupation of
Devata Island. He is not a general. He is not a strategist by training or by choice. He is a pedanda
— a Brahmin high priest whose family served the royal bloodline of Devata Island for
generations — forced into a role the occupation created and his theology never prepared him for.

He is old. He has been doing this longer than most of his rebels have been alive.

He knows more than anyone on the island about the mask, the contract Baradha built, the
cosmological mechanics of what Rangda is and what the Barong was meant to be. He inherited
that knowledge from his father, who inherited it from his father, across a chain of men who
preserved a priesthood for a kingdom that no longer existed and a king who never came.

What he did not inherit was certainty. Everything he knows about the mask he knows
secondhand. He has memorized the theology perfectly. He has never seen it work. He spent
decades feeding men to an altar on faith alone — faith in his grandfather's accounts, faith in his
father's accounts, faith in a contract written a thousand years ago by a holy man he never met.

When the prince survives, Wikananda feels two things simultaneously that he will never fully
reconcile: relief so enormous it unmoors him, and guilt so deep it has no bottom. The mask finally
chose. Every man who died before this was the cost of finding out it could.

He does not know how to live inside both of those truths at once. He has been trying for the
entire game.

## Appearance

Wikananda is visibly, completely old. Not frail — the distinction matters. He carries himself with
the upright bearing of a man who has performed sacred ceremony his entire life, who has held
posture as a form of spiritual discipline for so long that his body no longer knows how to abandon
it. But the age is there in everything else. The depth of the lines. The particular quality of stillness
that belongs to someone who has learned to conserve everything.

He wears the white of a pedanda — but worn, washed too many times in conditions that were
never meant for proper priestly maintenance. Two hundred years of occupation means two
hundred years of his lineage performing the role of royal priest in hiding. The white is correct in
form. The wear tells the real story.

His hands are the most telling part of him. A pedanda's hands are instruments — mudra, tirtha
preparation, the bell, the ritual gestures that are the physical vocabulary of his entire vocation.


Wikananda's hands still move with precision. The muscle memory is so deep that even now, even
tired, even under Rangda's corruption degrading his connection to Siwa, his hands know what to
do. Watching his hands is watching the only part of him that has never doubted.

He has a tell. When the weight gets too heavy — when a conversation goes somewhere he hasn't
prepared for, when someone asks the question he doesn't have the answer to — his right hand
moves. Not a gesture, not a mudra. Just his thumb pressing against his first two fingers, briefly,
and releasing. Like reaching for a prayer bell that isn't there. He does not know he does it.

## Background

**The Family**

Wikananda's family served the royal pedanda role for Devata Island's kings across generations.
When King Dharma fell — when Rangda walked into the palace and the occupation began — his
ancestor made the choice to stay. Not to flee, not to submit, but to remain on the island and
preserve what could be preserved.

That choice passed down like everything else. The genealogical records. The ritual knowledge.
The account of Baradha's contract, the mask, the cost written into it. The understanding of what
the Barong was and what it required. And alongside all of that — the altar, and the terrible
arithmetic of what the mask needed, and the responsibility for finding it.

**What He Inherited**

Wikananda knows the theology of the mask better than anyone alive. He can recite the terms of
Baradha's contract, explain the atma fracture mechanism, describe the cosmological relationship
between Barong and Rangda with complete precision.

He has never seen any of it function. His grandfather's accounts described kings calling the
mask's power. His father's accounts described the rebellion's early attempts at the altar ritual.
Wikananda himself has only ever seen the mask kill.

He inherited the role of keeper before he was ready. His father died and left a gap and Wikananda
filled it because there was no one else and the records needed someone to hold them. He has
been performing absolute certainty for the pemangku beneath him ever since. Some days the
certainty feels real. Most days it is the most disciplined performance of his life.

**The Altar**

The ritual requires the right bloodline. This is what the records say. This is what Baradha's
contract specifies. Two hundred years of the rebellion attempting the ritual means two hundred
years of Wikananda's lineage evaluating candidates — tracing genealogies through occupation
and hiding and record loss, identifying men with sufficient claim to the royal bloodline, bringing
them to the altar, and watching them die.

Wikananda knows the count. He does not know the names anymore.


He knows when he lost them — not a single moment, but a gradual dissolution. The grief
repeated too many times becomes texture. The faces stopped staying. The names stopped
adhering. What remained was the number, clean and terrible, and the knowledge that each one
was his decision.

He prays at the altar regularly. He has done this for decades. He goes alone, performs what
incomplete rites he can manage — a pedanda cannot give these men the full ceremony they
deserved, cannot perform the ngaskara properly for the beheaded, cannot do what his vocation
demands for the people his vocation required him to sacrifice. So he does what he can. He
addresses them collectively. He does not use names because he no longer has them. He lights
nothing, carries nothing, brings only himself and the weight and the prayer that their sacrifice is
not permanent waste.

He does not know that one of the men in that ground is the prince's father. The man came ten
years ago. Was evaluated. Had the right bloodline. Died on the altar. Was buried with the others.
Wikananda cannot remember his face. He is part of the count now, unnamed like the rest.

## The Rebellion

Wikananda leads the rebellion by default and by necessity. Priests do not hold political office.
They advise kings, tend to the sacred, maintain cosmic harmony through ritual precision. The
occupation removed every structure that would have given the resistance a proper leader and
left Wikananda — the most respected figure among the survivors, the keeper of the knowledge
they needed, the only person who understood what the mask was and what it could do — holding
a role his entire training never covered.

He learned. Two hundred years of his lineage learned. But it never stopped being an
improvisation performed as certainty.

The pemangku in the rebel base are the backbone of everything. They do what pemangku have
always done — daily rituals, ancestor contact, maintaining the sacred routines, providing
spiritual counsel to a community that has been living in hiding for generations. They perform the
ceremonies of ordinary life under extraordinary circumstances because the alternative is losing
the ordinary life entirely, which is exactly what Rangda wants.

Wikananda tends to them the way a high priest tends to lay priests — with authority and with
distance. He is their pedanda. He is also their leader. The roles sit uneasily on the same man and
he has never fully resolved the tension. He gives them what they need. What he cannot always
give them is honesty, because the full weight of what he knows would not help them and might
break them, and broken pemangku cannot perform the rites that keep the community intact.

## Relationship with Natha

Wikananda's relationship with Natha is the relationship his family has been building toward for
two hundred years without knowing who would fill the role. He knows what to do. He has the


knowledge, the records, the theological framework, the map of everything Natha needs to
accomplish. He gives it cleanly. He is a good guide.

What he is not is honest about the cost.

He knows about the atma fractures. He has watched enough candidates die mid-consumption to
understand what the mask takes from the person wearing it. He chose not to tell Natha
everything upfront — not from malice, from the same calculus he has always used. The mission
first. The full truth is a weight that could slow the vessel down, and the vessel needs to keep
moving.

Every time he looks at Natha he sees two things simultaneously: the proof that two hundred
years of sacrifice was not meaningless, and the person who has to pay the ongoing cost of it. He is
not equipped to fully reconcile those two things. He tends toward the mission because the
mission is what he knows how to serve.

He does not tell Natha about the count. He does not tell him about the names he lost. He does not
tell him that his father is in the ground at the altar where he woke up. He runs out of time before
any of those truths surface.

When Natha asks questions that go too close to the weight — the number, the altar, what the
mask has already taken from him — Wikananda's right hand moves. Thumb against first two
fingers. Releasing. He answers what he can answer and moves forward.

He never asks Natha to forgive him. Not once. In any version of his death. Whether this is dignity
or cowardice he does not know and has stopped trying to determine.

## Death

Wikananda dies in his chamber at the rebel base. Not in battle. Not by Rangda's hand. His body
simply stops — an old man who held on long enough to see the mask choose its vessel, long
enough to guide that vessel through most of the journey, long enough to know the palace assault
is ready and the ending is coming.

He dies during war preparation. The base is at its highest pitch of forward momentum — rebels
sharpening weapons, pemangku performing pre-battle rites, everyone moving toward the thing
they have been building toward for generations. In the middle of all of that, Wikananda's
chamber goes quiet.

Natha is called, or finds him, before the end. The conversation they have depends on where
Natha is.

**If Natha is heavily corrupted**

Wikananda looks at him for a long moment. Trying to find the person inside whatever the mask
has been doing. He asks — directly, without ceremony, stripped of the guide's composure — _are
you still in there._


He doesn't wait long for the answer. Whether it comes or not, he says what he came to say: that
whatever is in that vessel, whoever remains, he hopes it will be enough to end the witch's reign.
That the men in the ground at the altar deserve an ending. That he is choosing to believe the
mask chose correctly even now, even looking at what Natha has become.

He lets go. It is the most complete act of faith in a life built entirely on it.

**If Natha is neutral**

Wikananda looks at him with something that is not quite relief and not quite grief. He says he will
trust the rest to him. That everything Wikananda knows, everything his father and grandfather
preserved and passed down, everything that has been building toward this moment — it belongs
to Natha now. He is done carrying it.

He says he hopes he has been doing the right thing. Not past tense entirely — _hopes_ , present,
uncertain. The guide admitting out loud for the first time that he was not always sure he was
guiding correctly. That the arithmetic he performed at the altar was the best calculation he had
and not necessarily the right one.

He does not ask for absolution. He does not receive any. He closes his eyes with the sound of
preparation outside his walls.

**If Natha has cleansed the Barong**

Wikananda sits up slightly when Natha enters. He sees both of them — the prince and the
purified Barong, together, the way the mask was always meant to be carried. He has not seen this
in his lifetime. His grandfather described it. His father described his grandfather's description.
He is the first in his lineage to see it directly.

He speaks to both of them. To Natha first — to the man, to the person who carried the weight and
did not let it consume him. And then to the Barong — formally, as a pedanda addresses the
divine, with the full precision and authority of his role. The first time in two hundred years that a
pedanda of his lineage has performed this function correctly. The royal priest addressing the
divine protector of the land, the way it was always supposed to go.

He tells them both that he is confident. Not hoping — _confident_. That Natha and Barong together
will bring order to the land. That what comes after Rangda will be worth everything that paid for
it. That the island will have its golden age.

He dies with that certainty intact. It is the only version of his death in which the certainty is
earned rather than performed.

## After

The pemangku perform his rites. Full ceremony — as complete as they can manage under
occupation, which is more complete than anything Wikananda was ever able to give the men at
the altar. He spent his life performing incomplete rites for people who deserved better. His own
death receives the full weight of everything his community has left to give.


The preparation does not stop. It continues alongside the ceremony, because he would have
wanted that, because he spent his entire life refusing to let grief halt the mission, and the
rebellion honors him by doing the same thing.

Natha storms the palace carrying the last words Wikananda gave him. Every decision from the
gate onward belongs to Natha alone.

The altar is still there. The unnamed are still in the ground. Nobody living knows all their names.
That remains true after the ending regardless of which ending comes.
