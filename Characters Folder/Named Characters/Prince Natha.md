# The Prince

_Balinese Mythology Horror RPG — Protagonist Design_

Relation: [[Antara]], [[Wikananda]], [[Sayan]], [[Rangda]]
## Overview

The prince is the protagonist of a dark horror RPG set on Devata Island, a place rotting under
Rangda's dominion. He is not a hero. He is not chosen in any meaningful sense. He is a young
man who came to a cursed island looking for his missing father and became something he never
agreed to be — Barong's vessel, the rebellion's asset, a son who arrived too late.

He is the question at the center of the game's moral tension: can you do monstrous things for
right reasons? The narrative never answers this cleanly. Neither does he.

## Core Identity

**Who He Is**

An ordinary young man, roughly in his early-to-mid twenties. He grew up unremarkably —
farming tools, river water, the ordinary weight of a life with no reason to know violence. He came
to Devata Island because his father left ten years ago and never came home, and not knowing
was finally worse than the risk of finding out.

He is quietly perceptive. He notices everything — the way people look at him, the pauses in
conversations, the things that aren't said. He files all of it and says almost nothing. This is not
suppression. It is patience. He waits until the picture is complete before he moves.

**Core Tension**

```
Can you do monstrous things for right reasons?
```
This is the question he lives inside for the entire game. He never positions himself as a hero. He
doesn't have speeches or causes. He has forward motion and the faint possibility that if he keeps
going long enough, he will arrive somewhere that makes the journey make sense.

The horror is that he might be right. The horror is also that it might not matter.

**Psychological Profile**

**Quietly Perceptive** He reads rooms, people, and situations with precision. His perception serves
him well externally and fails him entirely on the question of what he himself wants. He can tell
what everyone around him needs from him. He cannot tell what he needs from himself.

**Functionally Dissociated Under Pressure** His hands were not shaking after waking on the altar.
That was the first thing that frightened him. He goes quiet and functional when things are worst
— not from courage, but from a psychological architecture that prioritizes forward motion over


self-inventory. This is his greatest strength and the exact mechanism of his destruction in the
Consumed ending.

**Grief With Nowhere To Go** He came for his father. He found an altar. What he carries afterward
is not grief with an object — it is grief with nowhere to put itself. He has an absence shaped like a
man and no material to fill it with. He moves through the rest of the game with this weight,
unexamined, unfiled.

## Background

**Before the Island**

His childhood was ordinary by design. He remembers it clearly — farming tools, river water, the
faces of whoever raised him. These memories are intact, solid, real. What he did not know is that
they are not the whole picture.

His father left ten years ago. No explanation that fully satisfied. Just — left, for reasons that
referenced something important without naming it, and then did not come back. The prince
carried this for a decade. The not-knowing accumulated until it became worse than the risk of an
answer.

He came to Devata Island alone, without telling anyone, to find out what happened to his father.
That is the entire reason. There is no other reason.

**Arrival on Devata Island**

The island was already wrong when he arrived. He could feel it — the decay, the wrongness, the
air itself carrying something that had no name in any language he knew. He went through
anyway because what else. He found someone. They talked. The person figured out he was an
outsider.

What happened next he does not fully remember.

He was led somewhere. There was an altar. Headless bodies arranged in a ring. He understood
what he was looking at and then they held him down and put something on his face and then
there was nothing until copper and dark ceiling and a blade his hand was already reaching for.

The rebels did not know who he was. They grabbed him because he was available and disposable
— a stranger, nobody's son in that moment, someone no one would miss if the mask consumed
him like it consumed everyone else. They ran before they saw what happened. They assumed
another corpse.

What walked out was not a corpse.

**His Father**

His father came to Devata Island ten years ago. He found the rebellion, worked with them,
believed in what they were trying to do. He tried the mask.


The mask recognized his royal blood — enough to identify lineage, not enough to hold. He had
the right bloodline and desperation instead of wholeness. The mask rejected him and then
consumed him, preserving the bloodline for the son it had already decided to wait for. The rebels
beheaded him because there was no other option.

He is one of the headless bodies circling the altar where his son woke up. The prince lay next to
him and did not know.

The priest cannot remember him specifically. He is one of countless. The rebellion ran so many
people through that altar across so many years that the individual faces stopped being faces and
started being attempts. The prince's father died nameless in someone else's accounting.

He was loved completely by one person. His son. Who wasn't there.

## Relationship With The Mask

**What The Mask Is To Him**

He does not know. That is the honest answer for most of the game. He woke up with it warm
against his temple, heard it call him young prince, felt it ask _let me_ before it took over — and has
been assembling the picture ever since.

He does not trust it. He does not distrust it. He treats it the way he treats the rebels — cautiously.
Listens to what it says. Does not assume it is lying, does not assume it is telling the whole truth.
Files everything and waits.

**The Ambiguity That Never Resolves**

Neither the prince nor the player ever fully knows whose will is whose. The mask asks
permission before acting — courtesy from something that has already decided. He says yes
because the spear is coming down and there is no other answer. He thinks about that yes for the
rest of the game. Whether it was a choice or a foregone conclusion wearing the shape of one.

Barong is not villainous. Barong is not benevolent. Barong is precise, patient, ancient, and correct
by every metric except the human one. The prince cannot argue that the mask makes wrong
calls. He can only note that being right while being cold is somehow worse than being wrong.

**The Cost of Resurrection**

When he died in the courtyard and came back, he felt strange. Incomplete. A small portion of his
soul — not a feeling, not a memory, the substance of what he is — was consumed by the lion as
the price of resurrection.

He noticed. He kept moving.

He does not know that every subsequent resurrection costs the same. Each time he comes back
he feels slightly further from the shore, slightly more muffled, reaching for himself through a
wall that used to be open air. He files it. There is always a later.


The mask never mentions the cost. He never asks. There is a tacit agreement between them that
neither party has explicitly made — he keeps moving, the mask keeps him alive, and the price
stays unexamined in the space between them.

**What The Mask Says**

Almost nothing. It speaks when it must and not before. Its first words were _I will not let you die.
Not yet, young prince. You have a duty still unmet._ Its most devastating words, delivered after the
worst moment of his life:

```
Fulfill your duty, young prince.
```
Four words that contain everything wrong with what the mask is. His father is nameless in a pile
of nameless dead. The priest cannot remember the face. The prince just broke down in front of a
stranger and came up empty. And the mask says: get back to work.

The mask is not wrong. That is the part he cannot stop returning to.

**How The Relationship Changes**

The mask's relationship with the prince is not fixed. It shifts based on how many times he dies —
how many soul fragments have been paid, how much of him remains, how much of the lion has
filled the space left behind.

**If He Dies Frequently — The Seduction** Each resurrection takes another fragment. Each
fragment makes Barong's voice feel less alien, less external, more like his own thoughts. The
warmth at his temple stops feeling like a presence beside him and starts feeling like something
inside him. He stops noticing the difference between what he wants and what the mask wants
because the border between them has been quietly dissolving for the entire game.

By endgame the seduction is complete — not through force, not through deception, but through
accumulation. He is genuinely convinced that the throne is his purpose, that the lion's hunger is
his own ambition, that the wanting was always his. The mask does not need to possess him. It has
become him, payment by payment, in the space he left unexamined.

**If He Dies Rarely — The Parasite** The mask stays external. A weight at his temple, a voice in his
skull, a power he borrows reluctantly and returns to arm's length afterward. It never feels like his.
It never stops feeling like a curse wearing the shape of a blessing — divine, necessary, and
entirely not him.

Limited integration means limited power. He cannot access Barong's full strength. He can
contain Rangda, chain her, push her back — but not unmake her. The cycle reconnects. The
kingdom breathes. The mask waits for the next vessel.

**If He Confronts The Lion — The Partnership** He finds the hidden entrance. He collects what is
required. He walks into the lion's den and fights Barong itself — not with the dead kings'
borrowed footwork, not with divine light flooding through him, but as himself. Conscious,
present, tired, and refusing to be either consumed or merely carrying something.


The lion does not fight him out of malice. It fights him because this is the only language it has for
_prove it._ He proves it. Not through strength — through completeness. Enough of him remains,
after everything the island took, to stand in front of a god and not flinch and not surrender.

The lion recognizes this. The warmth at his temple changes quality — stops feeling like a tide and
starts feeling like something standing beside him. Full access, full self. Not possession.
Partnership. The only vessel in the mask's history who earned it rather than being spent by it.

## The Ancestral Memories

**What Bleeds In**

As the game progresses, the dead kings whose memories live in the mask begin bleeding into
him — not as voices, not as conversations, but as glimpses. Wordless and specific. A king's hands
on a railing overlooking a valley that is now ash. A child running through a market that no longer
exists. The smell of something the island used to have and doesn't anymore.

He cannot push back against a glimpse. He cannot debate a memory. It arrives and it is gone and
he is left holding the feeling without the source — the specific grief of someone watching their
birthright rot, the weight of a kingdom that was supposed to be protected and wasn't.

**How The Ambition Grows**

The glimpses accumulate into a picture he assembled himself from pieces that were never his. By
the time he understands that he wants to restore the kingdom, he believes the wanting is his. He
built it from his own conclusions. The fact that the raw material was someone else's memory
doesn't make the conclusion feel borrowed.

The birthright doesn't arrive as ambition. It arrives as responsibility. He looks at the island — the
decay he walked through when he first arrived, the people hiding underground, the wrongness
in the air — and he can see the shape of the wound. He can see what is missing. The ancestors
showed him what it used to be. His own eyes show him what it is now. The distance between
those two things is what he decides to close.

**What He Cannot Separate**

His father's mission. The ancestors' ambition. Barong's agenda. His own grief. By midgame all
four are pointing the same direction and he has stopped trying to audit which is which. They are
all real. They all converge. That is enough.

In the Dominated ending, standing before the lion, this is part of what he proves — that he knows
the difference. Which wanting is inherited, which is installed, which is genuinely, irreducibly his.
The lion does not want someone free of all those influences. It wants someone who can carry
them all and still know where they end and he begins.

## Motivations


**What He Thinks He's Doing**

For most of the first half of the game: looking for his father. That is the whole reason. The island,
the rebellion, the mask, Rangda — all of it is terrain between him and an answer about a man
who left ten years ago.

When the answer arrives in the worst possible form, the mission does not end. It changes shape.
He is no longer looking. He is finishing something.

**The Midgame Turn**

He does not know why he keeps fighting, all the way until midgame. He only knows that if he
keeps going he will find an answer. That is not hope. It is something more skeletal than hope —
the refusal to stop before the picture finishes assembling.

The turn happens quietly. Not a revelation. Not a speech. A moment where he consciously
chooses to continue, understanding for the first time what he is choosing and why. His father
came here, tried, failed, died. The mask waited for the son. He is going to do what his father
couldn't — not for the island, not for Barong, not for the rebellion.

Because his father deserved someone who came back for him. And this is the only form that is
still possible.

That is not the same as his father coming home. He knows it is not the same. He chooses it
anyway.

## Relationship With The Rebellion

**How It Starts**

He owes them nothing. They grabbed him off the street, dragged him to an altar full of headless
bodies, held him down, put a mask on his face, and ran when it worked. They used him because
he was available and disposable. They left him there.

Whatever alliance forms between them is built on top of this. He knows it. They know he knows
it. Nobody says it directly because they need each other and the situation is too urgent for
honesty.

**The Dynamic**

The rebels think he is committed. The priest thinks he is destiny. Barong thinks he is doing his
duty. None of them are seeing what is actually there — a young man who keeps walking because
stopping would mean admitting there is nowhere to walk to.

He has leverage. He is the only one who can do what needs doing. He has vulnerability. They are
the only ones who might know something about his father. Neither side is fully honest. He is
managing them as carefully as they are managing him, and he is quieter about it.


**The Confrontation With The Priest**

When he discovers the mask only works on royal blood and assembles what that means about his
father, he goes to the priest. He is very calm. That calmness is the most dangerous thing in the
room.

He does not raise his voice. He states what he knows — _the mask only works on royal blood_ — and
watches which way the priest moves. The priest confirms it. Knew from the moment the mask
stayed. Did not tell him because the mission needed him functional.

Then the prince says: _My father._ Two words. The priest cannot remember him specifically.
Countless people went through that altar. The faces stopped being faces somewhere along the
way and the priest has been drowning in the guilt of that ever since.

The prince breaks down. Not rage. Not accusation. The specific collapse of someone who ran out
of the thing that was keeping them upright. When it passes he looks at his hands. He stands up.
He says, quietly:

```
I'm not doing this for the island.
```
Not a threat. Clarity. He wants the priest to know exactly what is driving him. Then he walks
away without waiting for a response.

**The Priest's Second Conversation**

The priest does not chase him immediately. He waits. Then finds him somewhere in the cavern
— not a formal setting, not a speech podium, just two people in a corner — and says what needs
saying without ceremony.

He does not invoke destiny or the gods' will. He is too guilty for that framing and the prince is too
perceptive to receive it. Instead he lays out the situation with complete honesty — what Rangda
is, what she has done to this island across centuries, what remains, what happens if nothing
changes. Not to the kingdom abstractly. Specifically. The people in this cavern. The children born
into hiding. The generations that will inherit this rot if the rot is not stopped.

And then, quieter:

```
You can leave. The mask cannot force you and I will not try. But you have seen this island. You
have walked through what she made of it. If you go, that is what remains. Not for a generation.
Forever. She has already won once. She will not leave room for another attempt.
```
Not a guilt trip. A fact delivered without heat. The priest is too tired for manipulation. He is just
telling the prince what the math looks like.

Then, as close to a gift as he can offer:

```
Your father stood where you are standing. He looked at the same island. He made the same
choice. I do not know if that means anything to you. I think it does.
```
The prince listens. Does not respond. Gets up eventually and goes back to work.


That is the scene. No music. No dramatic turn. Just a young man who came for one reason being
handed a second one he cannot argue with and cannot put down.

## Design Notes

**What He Is Not**

He is not a chosen one. He is not a hero who does hard things. He is not a villain with good PR. He
is not tortured in the way that telegraphs its own guilt. He is not defined by the mask. He is not
defined by his father. He is a question the game asks and does not answer.

**His Tell**

When he has done something he cannot undo, his eyes go to his hands. They are always
unmarked. He always looks anyway. The player will learn to read it before the game names it.

**His Voice**

He speaks rarely and precisely. He asks questions he already knows most of the answer to. When
he says something true it arrives without preamble or explanation. The most important things he
says are short — _I'm not doing this for the island. My father. Let me finish this._

**The Central Irony**

The mask chose correctly. It found the exact person capable of carrying this — someone who
pushes through, who notices and keeps moving, who will not stop before the picture finishes
assembling. These are precisely the qualities that make him effective. They are also precisely the
qualities that make the Consumed ending possible.

Barong did not choose a hero. Barong chose someone who would not stop. The difference only
matters if he realizes it in time.
