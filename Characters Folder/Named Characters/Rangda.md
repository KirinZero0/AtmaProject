# Rangda

## The Widow-Witch Queen

Relation: [[Ki Luruh]]

## Overview

Rangda is not a villain who chose evil. She is a mother who was answered with betrayal — and
spent a thousand years becoming something that no longer remembers the difference.

She rules Devata Island not from ambition but from consequence. The kingdom feared her. The
kingdom used that fear as a weapon. The kingdom turned her daughter's wedding night into an
ambush, and then stood over the body and called it justice.

What came after is what the prince must face.

She is not evil in the way that is simple to understand. She is evil in the way that grief becomes
when it has no object — when the person who caused it dies, and the grief has nowhere to go, and a
thousand years pass with nothing to spend it on except the world that made it.

The prince is not fighting a monster. He is fighting what remains of a woman who had something
worth protecting, and lost it, and never recovered.

## Background

### Before the Island

Rangda came to power as the Calonarang — the widow-witch whose daughter, Ratna Manggali,
was denied marriage because no family in the kingdom would risk the association. The kingdom's
fear of Rangda was the thing that destroyed what she cared about most, and the kingdom did not see
the irony in this. They never do.

She had a suitor in mind for her daughter. Her daughter refused him. Rangda accepted this. What
she did not accept was the man who came after — sent by the holy man Baradha and sanctioned by
the king, a weapon shaped like a suitor, deployed precisely because Rangda had stopped her
campaign of terror. She had allowed hope in. That was the lever they used.

She stopped the terror. She allowed the wedding. Perhaps she was tired. Perhaps she believed that
something could still be saved.

She should not have hoped.

### The Wedding Night

On Ratna Manggali's wedding night, they came for her. Baradha and the king, moving through the
dark, counting on the softness that love had put in her. They had calculated correctly. Maternal love
was the vulnerability. They had planned around it like a seam in armor.

She sensed them. She transformed. Whatever grief had been in her that night became something
else — ancient, enormous, furious. Baradha and the king barely survived. The ambush collapsed.


Events moved quickly after that. A blade was raised. An old wound returned. Baradha, alone
against Rangda at full fury, lost.

Rangda killed the man her daughter had married.

Ratna Manggali threw herself in the way.

Rangda killed her daughter.

There is no version of this she recovers from. There is no aftermath in which she becomes anything
other than what she is now. The grief did not give her something to do with it. It gave her a
thousand years and an island and nothing else.

### The Contract

Baradha's last act was a spell cast into an ordinary lion mask — decorative, ceremonial,
meaningless until he made it mean something. The king put it on. Power flooded through him.
Rangda was driven back, wounded. Not dead.

What she did not know then — what she would learn over centuries of being pushed back, again
and again — was the cost written into the contract. Every time a king called on the mask's power,
the mask took something in return. Not blood. Not years. Fractions of the atma — the soul itself,
shaved away each time the power was drawn. The kings who held her back for eight hundred years
were paying for every victory with pieces of themselves.

She did not choose this arrangement. She did not need to. The contract did not require her consent.
It only required that the bloodline continue and that someone keep putting the mask on.

For eight hundred years, someone did.

## The Eight Hundred Years

For eight centuries, Rangda tried to take Devata and failed. Not because she was weak. Because
each king in the bloodline wore the mask and spent pieces of their soul to stop her, and the mask's
power was sufficient, and she could not overcome it.

She came back each time. The grief does not exhaust itself. It does not accept defeat as a permanent
condition. She returned across generations, across the deaths of kings, across the slow turning of the
island's history, and every time the mask met her and pushed her back, she learned something.
About the contract. About the cost. About what the mask was actually doing to the people who
wore it.

This is how she learned about the atma fractures. Not from the inside — she has never worn the
mask — but from watching what happened to the kings over time. From watching the quality of
their power change as they drew on it more. From understanding, eventually, what the contract had
built into itself.

She found this interesting in the way that a wound finds inflammation interesting. Not pleasure.
Recognition.

She did not know how to use this knowledge. The kings continued to wear the mask. She continued
to be driven back. The pattern held.


Until King Dharma.

## The Occupation of Devata Island

By the time the game begins, Rangda has ruled Devata Island for two hundred years. She does not
govern. She does not administrate. She occupies.

The puppet court exists because a court must exist — because an island needs hierarchy to function,
and Rangda needs the island to remain intact, and that requires the appearance of order. She has no
interest in the day-to-day mechanics of power. The court manages itself out of survival instinct. She
is present as a weight, not as a hand.

The undead soldiers are a statement of permanence, not strategy. They do not sleep. They do not
tire. They do not forget. This is what grief looks like given authority — it repeats.

The corruption spreading outward from the capital is not deliberate. It is ambient. It is what happens
when something this old and this wrong stays in one place long enough. The land does not flourish
under Rangda. The land rots. She does not notice. She has not been paying attention to the land for
centuries.

Two hundred years of occupation is not the same as eight hundred years of trying to occupy. It is
quieter. The fighting is over. There is no one left to fight her with the mask. The island is hers and
she is doing nothing meaningful with it, which is appropriate, because she is not here for the island.
She was never here for the island. She is here because the contract is not finished and the bloodline
is not dead and somewhere, eventually, another vessel will come.

She was right about that.

## Appearance

Rangda's true form is monstrous. She does not wear it in the throne room.

What sits on the throne is a porcelain doll — white as snow, beautiful as something that has never
been touched by weather or grief or time. The face is perfect and still. Its eyes are closed. They do
not open. They were not made to open. The doll does not need eyes, because the crown does.

The crown is gold and heavy and formal, and set into its centerpiece is a single eye — lidded, real,
wrong in the way that eyes are wrong when they appear somewhere a face should be. This is how
Rangda sees. Not through the doll's face but through the eye in the crown, which watches the throne
room with a steadiness that never blinks and never wavers and tracks movement the way a hunting
thing tracks movement. Anyone who has spent time in the throne room knows not to look directly
at it. No one has explained why. It is simply known.

At her waist, worn as a clasp at the sash, is a golden Rangda mask — her own face rendered in
metal, facing outward. It is always watching in the way that the crown eye watches: not
decoratively, but actually.

Her robes are regal — deep, rich fabric that speaks of courts and ceremony and a power old enough
to have its own aesthetic — but they are torn at the edges, tattered where they trail along the floor,


elegant in the way that ruins are sometimes elegant. Not neglect. The world simply cannot maintain
itself in her presence forever. Things around her degrade.

At the tips of her fingers are strings — thin as spider silk, nearly invisible unless the light catches
them — trailing outward into the dark of the throne room, into the corridors beyond, out through the
palace walls and across the island to whatever agents and soldiers and puppets she is currently
moving. She does not gesture when she controls them. The strings shift on their own, drawn by will
rather than motion. When she is working many at once the strings multiply and cross and the air
around her hands looks webbed.

Behind her, more strings. These go nowhere visible. They simply extend into the dark and do not
end. No one who has served in the court has ever asked about them.

She cannot leave the throne room. This is the price of the puppet architecture — to hold this many
strings across this much distance requires her to remain stationary, the center point of a web that
spans the entire island. She does not appear to resent this. She has not moved voluntarily in two
hundred years. She may have forgotten what it felt like.

## How She Operates

### The Dishonesty

Rangda tells herself a version of events that is almost true. In her telling, the kingdom was cruel and
she responded to cruelty. The ambush was unprovoked. The man her daughter married was a
weapon and deserved what happened to him. Ratna Manggali's death was the kingdom's fault —
they set the events in motion, they used her love against her, they killed her daughter as surely as if
they had held the blade themselves.

This is not entirely wrong. But it does not include the moment where she was the one who killed
her daughter.

That moment lives in her, but she does not look at it directly. A thousand years is long enough to
learn where not to look.

This is the dishonesty that matters: Rangda is not honest about the specific thing she is grieving.
She tells herself she is grieving injustice. She is grieving the moment her daughter interposed
herself between a weapon and a man, and Rangda could not stop in time.

She does not say this. She may not know it consciously. But it is the wound underneath all the other
wounds, and every act of destruction she has committed since that night is, in some basement level
of motivation, an attempt to make that moment not have happened by making the world that
produced it cease to exist.

This is impossible. This is why she is still here.

### The Court

She does not like her court. She does not like anyone in it. They are useful, and usefulness is the
only currency she recognizes anymore.

Devotion, in Rangda's experience, is the thing people offer you right before they use it as a weapon.


Anyone who tries too hard earns her least interest. She observes. She does not teach. She does not
explain the difference to anyone who assumes otherwise.

### The Ritual Cycle

The Barong-Rangda dance performed at crossroads throughout the island is not something Rangda
ordered. It predates her occupation. It predates the specific form of her grief. The priests who
perform it understand something about the cosmological architecture of this place that even Rangda
does not fully understand — which is that the balance is not optional. Neither force can be
permanently destroyed without consequences that neither wants.

She knows this. She does not like it. She has, over centuries, made peace with it the way you make
peace with a law of physics — not through acceptance but through the gradual exhaustion of all the
alternatives.

## What She Is Dishonest About

Rangda is dishonest about four things, in descending order of her own awareness:

**The kingdom.** She tells herself her campaign of terror was proportionate. It was not. She knew it
was not. She continued anyway because the destruction gave the grief somewhere to go, and the
grief was going to go somewhere regardless.

**The ambush.** She tells herself the king had no legitimate grievance against her. He did not. She
does not include in this accounting the people she killed to make that point, or what their deaths
cost the kingdom in terms of having no good options left.

**The man her daughter married.** She tells herself he was nothing but a weapon deployed against
her. He was also a man who fell in love with her daughter and chose, at the end, to not choose her.
Both of these things are true. She only holds the second one.

**Ratna Manggali.** She does not talk about this. She does not think about this. A thousand years is
long enough to build very strong walls around a very specific memory. The wall does not mean the
memory isn't there.

## What She Wants

She wants to end the contract.

Not consciously. She would not say this if asked. What she would say is that she wants the mask
destroyed, the bloodline ended, the island secured. These are the stated objectives. They are also
true.

But underneath them is something simpler and harder to admit: she is tired. A thousand years of
grief and eight hundred years of being pushed back and two hundred years of occupying an island
that means nothing to her is an exhausting way to exist. The only thing keeping her here is the
unfinished quality of the wound. If the contract ends — if whatever cosmological bookkeeping
system arranged this decides she is no longer a participant — she can perhaps, finally, stop.

She does not know she wants this. But she does.


## What Remains

Somewhere inside the thing she has become is still the woman who stopped her terror campaign
because her daughter was getting married. Who allowed hope in. Who should not have hoped, but
did, because the alternative was acknowledging that something inside her still wanted a world
where her daughter could be happy.

That woman is not accessible. A thousand years is a long time to bury something.

But she is in there.

_"She did not set out to become this. She set out to protect one person, and failed, and has been
trying to find the appropriate scale of response ever since."_
