# Cakra

## The Village Chief — Elder of the Shore

Relation: [[Prince Natha]], [[Rangda]]

---

## Overview

Cakra was the elder and protector of the Shore village. Not a warrior by title — a protector in the older sense, the man the village turned toward when the world pressed in. He held things together under the occupation through the only tools available to someone with no army and no leverage: patience, presence, and the faith that the savior would come.

He waited a long time. The savior did not come. The occupation ground on and the Gatekeeper's threat sat over the village like weather — do anything, and everyone dies. Cakra watched his people bend under it for years and had no answer.

Then a stranger offered him one.

He took it. He did not know what it would cost. By the time he understood, the cost was already paid and what remained of Cakra was retreating into the temple to make sure it did not hurt anyone.

It hurt someone anyway. It was always going to.

---

## Appearance

**Before**

An old man who did not look old in the way that suggested fragility. The Shore is physical work — fishing, hauling, the constant maintenance of a coastal life — and decades of it had made Cakra the kind of elder who still moved like someone younger and still expected to be taken seriously, and was. His face was the face of someone who had been present for too many hard things and had not looked away from any of them.

He wore the dagger at his hip the way people wear things they have worn for so long they have stopped noticing. The Shore's dagger tradition runs deep — devotion to the Dagger King, the blade as both tool and sacred object. Cakra learned the forms as a child and had never stopped practicing. His hands knew the dagger before his mind caught up to any situation that required it.

**Now**

Monstrous. The transformation is complete — there is nothing readable as the elder in what stands in the temple. The bulging eyes of spirit world corruption. The pale, wrong skin. The bone protrusions pushing through at the shoulders and jaw and spine, the skeleton rearranging itself into something the body was never designed to be.

He is enormous. The spirit beast consumption built the body upward and outward in ways that make the temple feel smaller than it is.

The dagger is still at his hip. Too small for what he is now. He still reaches for it.

---

## Background

Cakra led the Shore village through the occupation the way you lead through something you cannot stop — by keeping people intact enough to survive it. The Gatekeeper arrived and made the terms clear: the village produces, the village complies, the village does not act against the kingdom's interests, and in exchange the village continues to exist. This was not a negotiation. It was a statement of conditions delivered by someone with the force to enforce them.

Cakra had no counter to it. He knew it. He accepted the terms because the alternative was the village ending, and a living village under occupation was better than no village. He told himself this was wisdom. Some days he believed it.

What he could not make peace with was the waiting. The Shore's faith is old — devotion to the Barong, the prayers for the masked king's return, the belief that the savior will come to restore Devata when the cycle demands it. Cakra had held that faith his entire life. He had carried it through the occupation as the one thing Rangda could not confiscate. But faith held under enough pressure begins to change quality. It stays faith, but it becomes an angrier kind.

He had been waiting for a long time. Devata had been suffering for a long time. The savior's silence had gone on long enough that Cakra's prayers, in his private moments, had started to carry an edge.

*How much longer.*

---

## The Stranger

Someone came to the Shore village. Not Rangda's soldier, not a rebel — a stranger, passing through, the kind of person who moves through occupied territory without explanation and without trouble. He spoke to Cakra privately. He knew what Cakra was looking for — protection, power, something that could stand against the Gatekeeper without the village paying for it.

He told Cakra about spirit beast consumption. Immortality. Strength that no ordinary force could overcome. He described it as a tool, as a means, as exactly the thing Cakra had been praying to find.

He told Cakra which spirit beast to hunt and how.

He did not mention the cost. Whether this was ignorance or omission is a question the stranger has not been made to answer yet.

*[The stranger is one of Rangda's disciples — field research, consistent with the Cliff Temple experimental program. The Gatekeeper holds the village hostage; the disciple uses that cover to run an unprocessed consumption test on the chief. Two arms of the same operation. Details to be written separately.]*

Cakra hunted the spirit beast himself. Alone, in the forest at the edge of the Shore's territory, at night. He found it. He brought it back. He consumed the meat in the temple, kneeling, and waited to feel what he had been promised.

The change began within days.

---

## After

The villagers saw him enter the temple. They saw him kneeling there for days, barely moving, not eating, not speaking to anyone who approached. They assumed it was prayer — Cakra had always taken his devotion seriously, and extended meditation was not unusual for the chief. They left offerings at the temple entrance. They waited.

He understood what was happening to him before the transformation made it visible. The wrongness was internal first — the consciousness retreating, the body beginning to act on impulses that were not his. He had enough of himself left to understand what he was losing and to know exactly what the stranger had not told him.

He stayed in the temple because it was the only thing he could still decide. He could not reverse what was happening. He could not undo the choice. But he could keep himself in one place, away from the village, away from everyone who trusted him. He could give them that much.

A villager came to check on him. The kindness of it — someone worried, someone who cared enough not to leave him alone — arrived at the wrong moment. The body that used to be Cakra was past the point of choosing.

The villager did not return.

---

## The Villagers

They know something is wrong. The village has not seen Cakra emerge from the temple in weeks. They know the villager who went to check on him never came back. They are not naive about what this means.

They are loyal anyway. Not because they don't understand — because Cakra was their protector for decades, and the thing in the temple is still, somewhere underneath everything the consumption built around it, the man who kept them alive under impossible conditions. They cannot separate those two things. They have not tried.

When Natha arrives, they urge him toward the temple, but not without words: *do not kill him. Find a way to bring him back. He went in there to protect us.* They believe this. It is true.

What they do not fully understand — what Natha may also not fully understand going in — is that the liberation available to Cakra is the same liberation available to the Dedari. Destroying the material body is not killing him. It is the only path back for what remains.

Whether this is comfort or not depends on how much of Cakra is still there to receive it.

---

## Encounter

The temple is dark when Natha enters. The offerings at the entrance are old — the villagers stopped bringing them inside after the villager disappeared, and left them at the threshold instead. The interior smells wrong. There is a sound somewhere in the dark that is not prayer.

Natha sees Cakra before Cakra sees him. What he sees first is what Cakra is doing, and then what Cakra has become, and both arrive at the same time.

Then Cakra turns.

The mask catches whatever light exists in the temple. Something happens in the monstrous face — not recognition, exactly. Not language. But something. A man who spent decades praying for the Barong's vessel to return, who built his entire final chapter around that faith, who consumed a stranger's poison because he could not wait anymore — that man, buried under everything the corruption built, registers the mask.

The material body does not stop. The grief and rage that have been accumulating in the corrupted form — the anger at how long Devata has suffered, the fury of a protector who became the thing his village needed protection from — all of it comes forward.

He still reaches for the dagger. The muscle memory is deeper than the corruption. His hands know the forms before anything else does.

---

## The Fight

Cakra fights with the dagger — not because he chose it, but because decades of practice live in his hands below conscious access. The forms are still there. The speed is wrong for what he is now, scaled up, the dagger too small for the body wielding it, but the technique is intact. He fights like someone trained and then made into something the training was never designed for.

The moments of recognition come during the fight. Not at predictable intervals. When the mask catches the light at a certain angle. When Natha moves in a way that matches something Cakra spent years imagining the savior would move. The monstrous form pauses — not long, not controllably — and something behind the corrupted eyes is briefly, visibly present.

He is in there. He has been in there the whole time. He cannot reach the surface.

The anger is real too. The grief underneath the anger is real. He waited too long. He broke the wrong way. The savior came and the first thing the savior has to do is fight him. Something in what remains of Cakra finds this unbearable and has no way to say so.

---

## After the Fight

Cakra does not return to himself. He goes unconscious — the body overwhelming finally by what Natha puts into the fight — and stays that way.

The villagers are ready. They have been ready. Rope, chains, the storage room behind the temple they quietly cleared out days ago when they understood what was coming and chose to prepare for incapacitation rather than death. They move quickly. By the time Cakra's body could stir again, he is quarantined — sealed away, contained, alive.

He is still the chief of the Shore village. He is imprisoned by the people who are still loyal to him. Both things are true and neither cancels the other.

The Dagger King Statue in the village square unlocks. The mask recognizes the Shore's sacred tradition — the dagger forms still intact in Cakra's hands even after everything the consumption did to him, the devotion to the Dagger King persisting in the muscle memory of a man who can no longer access himself.

The dagger manifests. Natha carries it forward.

Cakra stays in the quarantine room. The villagers leave food he does not eat and water he does not drink, because they do not fully know what he needs anymore and they are not ready to stop leaving it. Someone checks on him every day. They do not go inside.

He will remain this way until the veil is restored. If it is restored. The consumed do not know they are waiting. But they are waiting.

## Notes

**The stranger** who offered Cakra the spirit beast meat is an unresolved thread — his identity and motive are to be written separately. His knowledge of spirit beast consumption and its effects, combined with his apparent willingness to give that knowledge to a desperate village elder without disclosing the cost, places him as a figure of deliberate interest in the wider narrative.

**Cakra as contrast to the Middle Ground:** The villages of the Middle Ground consumed spirit beast meat unknowingly, tricked through the food supply. Cakra chose it — with incomplete information, yes, but chose. The consumption was the same mechanism. The tragedy is different in shape. One is systematic. The other is personal.

**The villagers' request** — to incapacitate rather than kill — is the correct instinct and the correct outcome. They wanted their chief back. They cannot have him back yet. What they can have is him alive and contained, which is the only form of hope available to them until the veil is restored. They understand this imperfectly. They act on it correctly anyway.
