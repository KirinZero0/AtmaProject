# Ki Luruh

**Rangda's Sisya — The Aspiring Student**

Relation: [[Rangda]]
## Overview

Ki Luruh is Rangda's most devoted student. Not her most powerful lieutenant. Not her most
trusted advisor. Her student — a distinction that carries everything about who Ki Luruh is and
what she wants.

She is ambitious, disciplined, and completely defined by a relationship that doesn't value her the
way she values it. She is not a villain in the way Rangda is a villain. She is something more human
and more sad than that — a person who chose devotion to the wrong thing and spent her entire
existence, including her second one, trying to earn recognition from someone incapable of giving
it.

## Appearance

**Before Death**

Ki Luruh is tall — noticeably, deliberately tall. She stands above the average soldier by enough
that it registers immediately in any room she enters. Not monstrous, not supernatural in scale.
Just a woman who occupies space with the kind of physical presence that makes people
recalculate before engaging her.

She wields an ornamental spear. Not decorative in the sense of fragile — ornamental in the sense
of significant. The engravings and adornments on the shaft and head mark it as a weapon with
history, with ritual meaning, with the particular weight of something that has been blessed or
cursed or both. It moves like an extension of her, which is the only way a spear that size works at
her reach.

Her face is half covered by a Rangda mask. Not a full mask — half. The exposed half is her own
face, readable, human. The masked half is Rangda's iconography rendered in carved material,
the bulging eye and the suggested fang pressed against her cheek. The split is deliberate. She is
Ki Luruh and she is Rangda's student simultaneously, and her face announces both without
apology.

Her hair is tied and controlled. Disciplined. A student performing discipline because discipline is
what students perform.

The armor is full — layered, heavy with Rangda's iconography, borrowed authority worn on the
outside. The chest piece carries Rangda's all-seeing eye, rendered in the center, always open,
always forward. It is not decorative. It functions. Rangda sees through it. Everything Ki Luruh


faces, Rangda can witness. Whether Ki Luruh finds this comforting or suffocating is a question
her expression never answers.

She looks like someone trying to look the part. Because she is.

**After Reconstruction**

The mask is gone. Her real face fully exposed — both halves, entirely hers, nothing borrowed
sitting on top of it. The face underneath is the same face. Somehow more itself without the mask
framing half of it.

The armor is less. Significantly less. She came back knowing she doesn't need it — not as
protection, not as statement. She's an expert now and experts don't perform expertise. What
remains is functional and minimal, the kind of loadout that communicates total confidence in
what the body underneath can do without additional layers.

Her hair is loose. Long, free, moving with her rather than contained by her. Something released
in the reconstruction that the first life kept tied down.

The cracks run across her skin and what armor remains — visible fracture lines where death left
marks that Rangda's magic sealed but couldn't fully erase. They don't glow exactly. They suggest
light from somewhere underneath, the seams of reconstruction visible the way a repaired
ceramic shows its joinery. She doesn't hide them. They're just part of what she is now.

The eye on the chest piece still blinks.

When the player sees her drop from the rooftop in the city center it takes a moment to register
who this is. The armor is wrong, the mask is gone, the hair is different. Then the spear catches the
light and something clicks.

She looks like herself for the first time. It's genuinely unsettling.

## Keris

Ki Luruh carries a Conduit keris — standard issue for Rangda's disciples, floating at her side, channeling the dark craft she has spent her existence studying. It is how she works magic. It is how she channels Rangda's methods into practical application.

It can also transform her. Not to the degree that Rangda's keris transforms Rangda — a disciple's transformation is not the same as the master's, and the gap between them is not something Ki Luruh has closed despite everything she has invested. But the potential is there. The keris knows what she is underneath the discipline and the borrowed iconography and the performance of devotion.

She does not use it in the first encounter. In the rematch, late in Phase Two, after the spear is already broken and the performance is already over and she has decided she has nothing left to spend — she uses it then. The keris drives into her heart. What comes forward is Ki Luruh with nothing left to hide.

It does not save her.

## Background

Ki Luruh came to Rangda willingly. The occupation of the island had already been complete for
long enough that an entire generation grew up knowing nothing else. For some that meant
survival under a corrupted court. For Ki Luruh it meant something else — she saw power,
genuine cosmological power, ruling the island and she wanted to understand it. To be near it.
Eventually to inherit some piece of it.

She became a sisya. A student of Rangda's craft — dark magic, dominion over spirits, the
particular art of unmaking things that Rangda has refined across centuries. She was talented.
Disciplined. Devoted in a way that most students aren't because most students have other things
in their lives. Ki Luruh had this.

Whether Rangda ever truly taught her or simply kept her close and useful is a question Ki Luruh
never asked herself honestly.


## First Death

When the mask activated for the first time in generations Rangda felt it immediately. A pulse
through the corruption, something waking up that should not exist anymore. She assessed the
situation and concluded — consumed vessel, standard threat, manageable.

She sent Ki Luruh.

Not as an insult. As a training exercise. The consumed vessels before had been feral, mindless,
powerful but directionless. Good practice for an aspiring student. Go handle it. Come back when
you're done.

Ki Luruh went. She found the prince. She killed him.

Then the mask resurrected him and she fought a human being who was learning in real time and
she underestimated what that meant.

She died on what was supposed to be a field exam.

## Reconstruction

Rangda rebuilt her.

Ki Luruh woke up in a body reconstructed by her teacher's magic, soul reseated in flesh that
wasn't quite hers anymore, and felt something she immediately buried underneath gratitude.

She thanked Rangda. Genuinely, completely, without reservation. Her teacher had pulled her
back from death. That meant something. That had to mean something.

The thing she buried was the question she couldn't ask — _why did you send me unprepared._

She never asked it. She built her second life on top of it instead.

## Second Life

Rebuilt, Ki Luruh trained. The prince was still out there, still wearing the mask, still alive. She had
the entire game's worth of time to prepare specifically for him. She studied what she
remembered of his fighting. She developed counters. She grew stronger than she was before she
died, which is either a gift from Rangda's reconstruction or a consequence of surviving
something that should have been permanent.

She told herself it was both. She told herself Rangda saw potential worth preserving.

The vengeance grew alongside the gratitude. Not replacing it — coexisting with it, which is more
dangerous. She didn't want to kill the prince because she hated him exactly. She wanted to kill


him because she believed that doing so would finally earn what she'd been reaching for since she
became a sisya.

Rangda's full recognition. The deeper secrets. The power that comes after proving yourself
worthy of it.

She believed if she brought Rangda the prince's death, everything would open up.

## The Rematch

The city center. The place that used to be the kingdom's heart, now decayed under Rangda's
occupation. The prince moves through it and Ki Luruh watches from above, picks her moment,
drops.

No monologue. She's done talking.

**Phase One**

She fights differently than she did in the tutorial. Disciplined, controlled, an expert
demonstrating expertise. Measured spear strikes. Calculated distance. She's studied what she
remembered of his fighting and developed counters for exactly the person he was the last time
they met — which means she hasn't fully accounted for who he's become since. The player's
muscle memory from the tutorial works against them. She's countered that version of the fight.

She's also auditioning. She believes Rangda is watching through the eye on her chest piece. She
believes this is the performance that changes everything between them. She fights like someone
who expects to win, cleanly and decisively, in a way that her teacher cannot miss.

**Phase Two**

When her health reaches half something settles in her that looks like calm and isn't.

The performance is over. The auditioning is over. The hoping is over. She looks at the prince and
something in her face goes quiet in a way that is more unsettling than rage.

She snaps the spear in two.

Not in frustration. Deliberately. The ornamental weapon with ritual significance, the one she's
carried her entire life — she breaks it across her knee and takes a half in each hand and comes
forward. No ceremony. No announcement. The weapon stops being a statement the moment she
decides she's done making statements.

Phase two Ki Luruh is faster. Closer range. Dual wielding the broken halves with a rhythm
completely different from the spear's reach — the player's adaptation to phase one works against
them again. She gets in, she hits, and when she hits she drinks. The prince's blood holding her
reconstruction together as the cracks spread under the strain of what she's spending.


She's not fighting for Rangda's recognition anymore. She stopped caring about the eye on her
armor. She stopped caring whether this earns anything or proves anything or opens anything up.
She just wants the prince dead and she's going to spend everything she has left making that
happen.

She has nothing to lose. Whether she wins or not doesn't matter to her anymore.

She just wants this one thing.

The tragedy is she almost deserves to get it.

## Permanent Death

The prince burns her soul.

Not her body — her soul. Knowledge earned somewhere on his journey, through a memory
fragment or a discovery in Segara's deepest corruption. Permanent death. Nothing for Rangda to
reconstruct this time.

The audience she was performing for felt it, paused briefly, and moved on.

Not callously. Just — moved on. The way you register losing something useful. A slight
recalibration. No grief. No rage on her behalf. Just the absence of Ki Luruh where Ki Luruh used
to be, and the business of everything else continuing.

She never got what she was looking for.

The thing she was devoted to didn't see her the way she saw it.

## Role in the Story

**Tutorial** — Kills the prince. Establishes the world is serious. Dies to the mask's resurrection and
the prince's second attempt. Departs believing the job is done.

**City Center** — Late game skill check. Returns stronger, more prepared, more desperate
underneath the discipline. Dies permanently.

**Thematic function** — Dark mirror to the prince. Both driven by ambition. Both in service of a
greater power. The difference is the prince's ambition belongs to himself. Ki Luruh's entire sense
of self runs through Rangda's approval. She is what the prince could become if he let something
else define his worth.

## On Rangda

Rangda was not cruel to her. That would have been easier.


She was simply not as invested as Ki Luruh believed. She kept the promise of greater power
vague enough to maintain the devotion without ever specifying what it would look like or when
it would come. Whether she intended to deliver on it at all is a question the narrative doesn't
answer because Ki Luruh never lived long enough to find out.

She was useful. She was talented. She was devoted in a way that made her reliable.

That's probably all she ever was to Rangda.

Ki Luruh spent two lifetimes trying to be more than that.

_"She thanked her teacher for rebuilding her. She never asked why she was sent unprepared in the
first place. Some questions are too dangerous to ask when the answer might unmake everything
you've built your life around."_
