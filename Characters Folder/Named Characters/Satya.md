# Satya

**The Hunter Who Stayed**

Relation: [[Prince Natha]], [[Wikananda]]

## Overview

Satya is not a fighter. He is not a rebel. He is a man who used to be a farmer, became a hunter out of necessity, and has been refusing to leave his village for two years because leaving would mean admitting there is no village left to come back to.

He is the first living person in the Grassland who will actually talk to Natha. Not because he trusts strangers — he doesn't — but because he has been waiting for someone capable of killing the Overseer, and Natha is the first person who has looked capable of it.

He is practical about this. He is not warm about it. There is a difference.

## Background

Satya grew up in the village at the Crossroads — the one that is now a slave operation running on people who used to be his neighbors. He knows every face. He knows the woman who used to argue with him over water rights. He knows the man who taught him to set traps. He knows the children, though the children are harder, and he does not think about the children if he can help it.

When Rangda's forces arrived and the corruption began spreading through the food supply — spirit world animals hunted and eaten by people who did not know what they were eating — Satya was in the forest. He came back to a village that had already started to change. He watched it finish changing over the following weeks.

He did not leave. He tells himself this is because someone needs to watch over them. This is partly true. The other part is that leaving would require a destination, and the only destination available is the Rebellion Base, and going there would mean accepting that the village is gone and the people in it are gone and what remains is something else wearing their faces.

He is not ready to accept that. He may never be ready. He is a hunter, and hunters are patient, and he has decided to wait.

**The Overseer**

Rangda's forces installed an Overseer to manage the slave operation — a human collaborator, not a soldier, someone who understood agricultural logistics and was willing to apply that understanding to a workforce that could not refuse. The Overseer is the reason the village continues to function as a supply line to Narendra. The Overseer is also the reason Satya cannot leave — not practically, not morally. As long as the Overseer is running the operation, the mutated villagers are being worked. Leaving means abandoning them to it.

He knows he cannot free them himself. He is one man with a bow and he has been watching the Overseer's guard rotation for two years without finding an angle that does not end with him dead.

When Natha arrives, Satya reads him the same way he reads any threat in the forest — quickly, from cover, without commitment. The mask is alarming. The way Natha moves is not. He decides to wait and watch before deciding anything.

## Meeting Natha

He does not approach Natha immediately. He observes from a distance that is close enough to act and far enough to retreat. When he finally speaks, it is direct: he knows what Natha is here for, he knows what Natha needs, and he has information that is worth exchanging for the Overseer's death.

He does not frame it as a request. He frames it as a transaction. He is not asking Natha to do him a favor. He is identifying a mutual objective and proposing cooperation. The distinction matters to him.

After the Overseer is dead he is quiet for a moment. Not grief — the Overseer does not warrant grief. Something more like the specific feeling of a weight that has been sitting on your shoulders so long you stopped noticing it, and now it is gone, and your body does not know what to do with the absence yet.

He accepts the base mantra without ceremony. He does not thank Natha. He says he will be there.

He is.

## At the Rebellion Base

Satya arrives at the Veil with his bow, his camp tools, and nothing else. He sets up in a corner of the base that has good sightlines to the entrance and begins making himself useful in the way hunters make themselves useful — quietly, practically, without asking for acknowledgment.

He sells bow techniques and supplies. The skills are real and earned. He teaches what he knows without performance or elaboration. The transaction is the transaction.

He does not talk about the village often. When he does it is specific — a particular face, a particular detail, something concrete rather than the weight of the whole. He has learned that carrying the whole is not sustainable. He carries the parts instead.

He believes the mutated villagers can be cured. This belief is not theological. It is not based on any evidence Wikananda has offered him. It is based on the fact that what happened to them was caused by something external — they ate the wrong thing, they did not choose this, it was done to them — and therefore it should be possible to undo. He holds this belief the way he holds his position in the forest: patiently, without announcing it, waiting for the information that confirms it.

He does not ask Natha about the possibility of a cure. He does not want to make it a question that can be answered no.

## Dominated Ending

When the veil heals and the corruption recedes, the spirit-meat mutations reverse. It happens all at once, across the island, in every village and field and road where someone ate the wrong thing and became something their family stopped recognizing.

Satya is in the throne room crowd when the people file in. He is near the back, not kneeling — not from defiance, simply because kneeling is not something he does easily and the moment does not seem to require it from him specifically. He is watching Natha on the throne with an expression that is difficult to name. Something between relief and the particular tiredness of someone who has been right about something for a very long time and is only now allowed to stop proving it.

The village will have its people back. Not the same people — two years of what they survived will have left marks that don't reverse with the corruption. But themselves. Still themselves.

He leaves the throne room before the ceremony finishes. He has somewhere to be.

## Design Notes

**What He Is Not**

He is not comic relief. He is not a warm mentor figure. He is not defined by grief in a way that makes him passive. He is a man doing the most functional version of waiting he can manage, and when the waiting is over he acts without hesitation.

**His Economy**

Satya does not waste words or movement. He says what he means in the minimum number of words that will be understood. He does not explain his feelings. He does not invite questions about his past. The information he offers is always practical. The information he keeps is always personal.

**His Belief**

The belief that the mutated villagers can be cured is the most human thing about him. It is not rational. It is not supported by the evidence available to him. It is simply the thing he has decided is true because the alternative — that they are gone and only the bodies remain — is not something he can build a life around. He is a hunter. He is practical. And this one thing he has refused to be practical about, quietly, without telling anyone.
