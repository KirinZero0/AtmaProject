# Ashwatthama

**The Wanderer**

Relation: [[Prince Natha]]

---

## Overview

Ashwatthama is the son of Drona — greatest weapons-master of the Mahabharata age. He fought on the side of the Kauravas. After the war was lost, he raided the Pandava camp at night and killed Draupadi's five sons. Then he fired the Brahmastra at Uttara's womb — targeting the unborn Parikshit, last heir of the Pandava line. Krishna neutralized it, revived the child, then stripped the gem from Ashwatthama's forehead and cursed him: he would not die. He would wander. The wound would never close.

That was roughly three thousand years ago.

He is on Devata Island for no particular reason. Wanderers go where they go. He has long stopped needing reasons.

He is not marked on any map. He has no quest indicator. He looks like a tired old man resting at the base of a tree in the Spirit Beast Zone. Players who do not speak to him will never know who he was.

---

## Appearance

Old. Not frail — just old in the way only something immortal can be, where the age is not decay but accumulation. Lean body, worn traveling clothes with no identifiable origin. Dust on everything.

The wound on his forehead is visible and permanent. Not bleeding. Not infected in any way a physician would recognize. Simply open — a shallow crater where the gem was torn free, the skin around it pale and settled. It has looked this way for three thousand years. He stopped noticing it long ago. He does not cover it.

He carries nothing useful. No weapon visible. Whatever he once was in battle, he is not performing it now.

---

## Personality

Flat. Not cold — flat. The difference is that cold implies suppression. There is nothing being suppressed. Three thousand years of wandering has not broken him or hardened him. It has simply worn all the peaks and valleys smooth.

He speaks plainly. He answers questions directly. He does not volunteer information unless he judges it worth the breath.

He is not wise in the way Wikananda is wise — full of preserved knowledge, careful about what he releases. Ashwatthama knows things simply because he was there when they happened, or because he has had three thousand years of nothing to do but think. The knowledge is just sitting in him, unguarded, available to whoever asks.

He finds Natha mildly interesting. Not because the prince is special — he has seen versions of this story many times — but because the mask situation is a specific kind of terrible that he recognizes. He will say so if asked.

---

## What He Gives

He will talk about:

- **His own fall** — the war, Drona's death, what he did after, what Krishna said. Not with grief. With the tone of someone recounting weather from three thousand years ago.
- **The Brahmastra** — the weapon that ended his honor. The curse that ended everything else.
- **Yuga cycles** — how the ages turn, how Kali Yuga warps dharma until right action becomes almost indistinguishable from wrong action. He does not say this to comfort Natha. He says it because it is true.
- **What happens when divine power bonds to a mortal** — he has seen it. Different masks, different gods, different mortals. The mechanism is always the same. He describes the end state without editorializing.
- **His opinion on immortality** — given only if Natha asks directly. Short. Final.

---

## On Natha

He does not tell Natha what to do. He does not encourage him or warn him off. He observes and, if asked, describes what he sees.

What he sees is a man being eaten by something that chose him because the choosing was convenient, not because he was ready.

If Natha is heavily corrupted when they speak, Ashwatthama looks at him for a moment longer than usual before answering anything. He does not say what he is looking for. The player can decide whether he found it.

He never gives his name unprompted. If Natha asks who he is, he says his name — once, plainly — and continues talking about whatever he was talking about. He does not explain why the name should matter. Either Natha knows or he doesn't.

---

## Dialogue Progression

Each conversation with Natha unlocks more. He does not withhold — he simply answers what is asked, and most people do not ask enough.

**First conversation**
Present tense only. Where he is, how long he has been sitting. If Natha asks about the wound, he says what it is without elaborating. If Natha asks about the mask, he looks at it and says he has seen something like that before.

**Second conversation**
The war. Drona's death. What happened after. The night raid. The Brahmastra. He recounts it in sequence, no grief, the way someone describes weather from three thousand years ago. If Natha asks why he did it, he says he does not know anymore. He has had a long time to think about it and the answer keeps changing.

**Third conversation**
After the curse. Centuries of walking. He describes India changing around him — kingdoms collapsing, languages dying, rivers rerouting. He passed through the time of the Buddha. Sat with him for a while. Listened to everything he said. He tells Natha that the teachings made complete sense and changed nothing about his situation. Not bitterly. Just accurately.

He also recognizes Natha's understanding of sekala and niskala — the visible and the invisible, material and spirit, divided. He says he knew this split by different names before Bali existed. Every culture he walked through built a different theology around the same crack in reality. Same two worlds. Different words for the seam between them.

He does not say what this implies about Rangda, the contract, the mask — that all of it is local. A regional story playing out inside something vast and much older. He lets Natha sit with that inference himself.

**Fourth conversation**
Rangda. Natha will have learned by now what she is and where her power comes from.

Ashwatthama tells him: Rangda's power came from Durga — Shiva's wife, the destroyer, the source. The power is real. The lineage is legitimate. This is not corruption pretending to be divine. It is divine power, correctly received, used in a direction Durga never specified.

He explains what he knows about gods and power: they do not track what their gifts become. Their understanding of good and evil is not a sharper version of human morality. It is a different axis entirely — one that does not map onto what mortals call right and wrong. When Durga gave that power into the world, she was not thinking about Rangda's grief or what it would do to a kingdom. When Brahma gave the Brahmastra to warriors, he was not thinking about infants.

He says this without self-pity. He fired a divine weapon at an unborn child and the weapon did not refuse him. The god who made it did not intervene. That is simply how divine power works. It is given. It is not monitored.

He lets Natha sit with what this means for the mask. For Barong. For whatever is happening to Natha inside it. Whatever Natha becomes, the divine source of that power is not watching. It is not going to stop anything on Natha's behalf because the use has become wrong.

The only thing that stops it is Natha.

**Fifth conversation**
What he actually thinks. About the curse. About whether it was just. About dying. He has no conclusion. Three thousand years and he is still not sure Krishna was wrong. He is not sure Krishna was right either. He stopped needing to resolve it.

If Natha asks what he wants, he is quiet for a moment. Then: *to finish walking, eventually.* That is all.

---

## Sekala / Niskala

When Natha uses the Balinese framing — sekala for the material world, niskala for the spirit world — Ashwatthama does not correct him or ask for explanation. He simply nods and uses the terms back.

He has heard it named: vyakta and avyakta. Rupa and arupa. Seen and unseen. The words change. The split does not. He has been crossing between the two for long enough that the boundary feels less like a threshold and more like a habit.

He finds Balinese cosmology coherent. Not better than what he grew up with — just coherent. Another people who noticed the same thing and built carefully around it.

What he does not say immediately — and will only say if Natha asks how he knows the split so well — is that he remembers when it did not exist.

In the age he was born into, rakshasas were not somewhere else. They were here. In the forest, on the road, at war alongside humans and against them. Gandharvas, nagas, apsaras — not mythological, not glimpsed through ritual. Just present. The way animals are present. You learned which ones were dangerous and which ones you could negotiate with, the same as anything else.

The split was not a creation. It was a drift. He watched it happen over centuries — the point where you stopped seeing rakshasas in the road without looking for them. The point where crossing required ritual instead of just walking in the right direction. The point where people began building theologies to explain a division that had not always been there.

He does not frame this as loss. It is simply what happened. The worlds pulled apart as Kali Yuga deepened, and the people born after the drift have no way to know the difference because they have nothing to compare it to.

Natha's understanding of sekala and niskala — the careful separation, the different rules, the need for specific passages between them — is a theology built entirely inside the drift. It is accurate to how the world is now. Ashwatthama is the only one who can tell him it was not always this way, and that the separation Natha treats as fundamental is, in cosmic terms, relatively recent.

---

## Location

Spirit Beast Zone — Forest Depth. Sitting against a large root, eyes open, doing nothing. He has been there for some time. He will be gone next time the player passes through, without explanation, as wanderers are.
