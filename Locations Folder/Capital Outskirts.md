# Capital Outskirts

### Location Document | Lv 3

---

## Overview

The Capital Outskirts is the settlement that grew outside Narendra's walls over generations — the overflow of a city that never expanded its gates to match its population. Merchants, laborers, families without the standing to live inside — they built here, along the road that leads to the capital gate, close enough to the city to depend on it and far enough outside to be the first thing sacrificed when the city needs to protect itself.

Under ordinary occupation, the Outskirts had a kind of life. Not comfortable. Not free. But present — markets, noise, the movement of people who had found the smallest available margin and were living inside it.

Natha arrives to find it silent.

Rangda knew he was coming. The order went out before he reached the cliff: everyone inside, gates closed, nothing moves in the Outskirts without uniform. The settlement has been converted into a military staging ground. The people who live here are behind their doors. The streets belong to Rangda's soldiers — and to what the soldiers have become.

---

## The Lockdown

The lockdown order is posted at every entry point to the Outskirts. Printed on official parchment, stamped with Narendra's seal — the same administrative apparatus Rangda co-opted from the kingdom she occupied. The language is bureaucratic. The order is clear.

*By decree of the Regent's Office: All civilian movement within the Capital Outskirts is suspended until further notice. Residents are to remain in their dwellings. Cooperation is expected and will be noted. Non-compliance will be addressed.*

The soldiers enforcing it are posted at intervals that leave no gap. The Patrol Grounds are exactly what the name implies — a maintained rotation, coverage of every approach road, no dead angles. Whoever designed the patrol pattern knew what they were defending against.

The Outskirts does not feel like a military operation. It feels like a city holding its breath.

---

## What the Disciples Did to the Guard

The Twin Gatekeepers are not the only product of what Rangda's disciples have been doing to the capital's military. They are the most visible result of a program that has been running since the Cliff Temple experiments established the proof of concept.

If spirit beast cells, properly processed, can be introduced into a human subject and produce something functional — something stronger, faster, more capable than what it was — then the question is not whether to apply this to the military. The question is which subjects and which source material.

The Rakshasa are giant beings of the spirit world — massive, predatory, built for force in a way that most spirit world beings are not. They do not cross into the material world often. When they do, the corrupted land builds them a body the way it builds any stranded being's body: from what is available, shaped like what they are. What it builds for a Rakshasa is enormous.

The disciples sourced the material. The capital guard provided the subjects.

The guard did not refuse. Refusal was not an option the subjects were offered — the framing was service, honor, the enhancement of Narendra's protectors. Some believed it. Some didn't have the standing to object. The process was presented as a choice in the way that choices look when the alternative is not being presented.

The results came out wrong in the right direction. The enhanced guards are massive — Rakshasa-proportioned, the original human frame buried under the bulk the cellular material forced on it. The face is still recognizable if you look long enough. Most people don't look long enough. The remnants of their guard armor sit on them now in pieces — pauldrons that barely fit one shoulder, chest pieces split along the sides to accommodate a torso that was not its original size when the armor was forged.

They retained function. They follow orders. They are still guards, in the essential sense — they stand at their posts, they respond to threats, they hold positions. The enhancement did not touch what they were trained to be. It only changed everything else.

## Area Breakdown

**I. Outskirt Road**

The approach from the Cliff Temple Summit descends into the Outskirts along the main road. The settlement is visible before Natha reaches it — rooftops, the wall of Narendra beyond, the gate tower rising above everything. The road is maintained. Empty. A patrol is visible in the middle distance before Natha enters the zone proper.

The contrast with the Middle Ground is immediate. The Middle Ground's corrupted villages were wrong in a quiet, ongoing way. The Outskirts is wrong in a taut, deliberate way. The wrongness here has been organized.

**II. Patrol Grounds**

The open area between the road entry and the settlement proper. No cover. Rangda's soldiers run a continuous rotation here — the most exposed section of the Outskirts, by design. This is where anyone approaching the capital is first seen and first engaged.

The enhanced soldiers are visible here alongside regular infantry. The size difference is unmissable. The patrol rotation places them at the anchors — the positions that hold when the lighter infantry moves. They do not pace. They stand. They watch.

**III. Barracks District**

The converted military staging area — former residential blocks reorganized into barracks, supply storage, and equipment maintenance. The original buildings are still the original buildings. The windows that used to show domestic interior now show stacked gear and firelight. Laundry lines have been replaced with weapon racks.

The soldiers stationed here are off-rotation — resting, maintaining equipment, receiving orders. It is the most inhabited-feeling section of the Outskirts, which makes it feel more wrong, not less.

The sewer entrance is here. A maintenance grate, not obviously significant, set into the ground between two barracks buildings. It is not guarded — the sewers are not supposed to be a concern. Players who investigate will find it accessible. Players who do not will not know it is there.

**IV. The One Open House**

On the edge of the Barracks District, one house has its door slightly open. Not an invitation — a detail that reads as oversight, or as someone inside who needed air and couldn't fully commit to closing it.

Inside: one of the settlement's residents. Older. Has lived here long enough to have watched the Outskirts change through every phase of the occupation and this phase is the one that finally looks like an ending.

He does not know who Natha is. He explains, without much editorial, that everyone is inside on order. Has been for days. The soldiers come by twice a day to confirm compliance. He does not know what they're waiting for. He knows it is something specific, because the order did not come with a duration — just a start and a *until further notice* that has so far not been further noticed.

He has a child's drawing on the wall behind him. Done on the inside of a flattened supply crate. The Outskirts market, the capital gate in the background, figures that are probably family members, colors used confidently and without accuracy. It was made before the lockdown. The child is inside somewhere in the settlement now, also under order.

He does not ask Natha to do anything. He is past the age where he expects the situation to respond to what he wants. He talks because it is the first conversation he has had in days and the door was already open.

**V. Capital Gate Plaza**

The ceremonial approach to Narendra's main gate. The plaza was built to impress — broad stone, the gate tower rising above, the scale of it designed to communicate the permanence and authority of what lies beyond. The occupation left the architecture intact because the architecture still serves the same function. Power reads power regardless of who is holding it.

The plaza is empty of civilians. Rangda's soldiers hold the perimeter. The gate is sealed.

At the center of the plaza, positioned between Natha and the gate: the Twin Gatekeepers.

They have been standing there since the order went out. Not pacing, not rotating. Standing. Waiting for exactly this.

**BOSS: Twin Gatekeepers**

They were the capital gate's assigned guard — the position of highest visibility and highest honor in Narendra's military hierarchy. The city's face. The first and last thing anyone saw entering or leaving the capital.

The disciples chose them specifically. Not random subjects. The gatekeepers. If the gate was going to be held against something that could not be held by ordinary means, it required guards that were not ordinary anymore.

They fight together. This is not a coordination they were trained for — it is something that emerged from the enhancement, a synchronization that has no military doctrine behind it. They move like they know where the other is without looking. Attacks that would create openings against one fighter create setups for the other. Fighting one means managing both. Fighting both at the same time means understanding a pattern that was not designed to be understood.

The remnants of the ceremonial gate armor are still on them — the formal livery of the capital's guard, in pieces now, sitting on frames it was never sized for. The insignia of Narendra's gate is visible on what remains of the chest piece. They are still, in some technical sense, doing their job. The gate is behind them. They are in front of it.

When they fall, the main gate to Narendra opens.

## The Sewer Route

**VI. Sewer Entrance**

The maintenance grate in the Barracks District. Drops into the sewer system running beneath the Outskirts and the capital wall.

**VII. Capital Sewers**

The capital was supposed to be clean. The corruption that turned the Middle Ground's villages into slave farms was a managed process — the villages were the intended vector, the capital's food supply was screened, the residents of Narendra were not supposed to be exposed. The occupation's internal logic required a functioning administrative class, a military that could follow complex orders, a court that could be intimidated rather than simply pointed at a field.

The sewers are not managed.

The people who were exposed — servants, workers, residents who came into contact with contaminated supply that the screening missed, or that was never screened because it moved through channels that did not officially exist — ended up here. Not thrown here deliberately. They came because the sewers are the one part of the capital's infrastructure that nobody is looking at. The Outskirts lockdown did not extend underground. The afflicted found the only space that had no order posted on it.

They are not aggressive in the purposeful way. They move the way the consumed always move — without direction, following impulse, the body acting on instinct in the absence of the consciousness that used to give it instruction. The sewers are dark and low-ceilinged and full of them.

**VIII. Wall Foundation**

The sewer system runs beneath the wall. A section of the foundation is compromised — old infrastructure, old repair work, the kind of structural weakness that exists in any city wall that has been standing for centuries and has not been fully maintained since the kingdom that built it fell.

The breach is not large. It is large enough.

On the other side: Narendra's Lower City. The sewer has bypassed the gate entirely. The Twin Gatekeepers are still standing at the plaza above. The gate is still sealed.

Natha comes up inside the walls.

## Lore Notes

The lockdown order uses the kingdom's own administrative language. Rangda did not invent a new bureaucracy — she inherited Devata's and kept it running, because a functioning bureaucracy is more useful than a destroyed one. The parchment the order is printed on carries the same watermark that Devata's kings used. The seal is different. The format is the same.

The Rakshasa enhancement program represents the disciples extending their research from logistics (Cliff Temple — flight for supply transport) to military application. The same core mechanism — spirit world cellular material introduced into human subjects — scaled toward force rather than mobility. The Cliff Temple established that it could work. The capital guard established that it could be made reliable enough to post at the front gate.

The sewer population is the capital's unacknowledged failure. Rangda's occupation presented Narendra as clean — uncorrupted, functional, proof that the occupation was sustainable. The sewers are where that narrative breaks. The consumed who ended up there were not supposed to exist. The official position is that they do not.
