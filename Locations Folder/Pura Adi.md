# Pura Adi

### Location Document | Hidden Area — The Threshold Temple

---

## Overview

Not a spirit world location. Not a material world location. The space in between — the boundary where the two press against each other — and within that space, a temple that has existed since before the boundary did.

Pura Adi was built by a civilization that predates the split between worlds. Their people did not need ritual or devotion to access the spirit world — they lived in both simultaneously, the way the living breathe air, without awareness that it could be otherwise. When the split happened, their world ended. The temple survived by being exactly where the split occurred: in the seam. It did not fall into either world. It got caught between them, and it has been there ever since.

Entrance is through the hidden gate in the cliff face of Shore Area 2. The door does not look like a threshold to another dimension. It looks like an old door. On the other side is somewhere else entirely.

---

## Atmosphere

Both worlds bleeding into each other — but not corrupted. Not wrong. Just *threshold*.

The architecture is material: stone, weight, proportion, the logic of a structure built to stand. But the light does not come from a source. The sound does not echo correctly — or rather it echoes the way things echo in the spirit world, off surfaces that are not fully present. Small material world details — moss, worn stone, the groove where countless feet have stood — coexist with details that do not belong to material space: depth that extends further than the structure should allow, a quality of stillness that the living world does not produce.

It is not hostile. It is not welcoming. It is a place that belongs to neither world and has never needed to perform either.

The air is thin in the way Communion Island's air is thin. The boundary is closest here. Not softened — simply absent. Pura Adi does not exist at the boundary. It exists instead of it.

---

## The Civilization

No name has survived. What has survived is their stonework — proportioned for people significantly larger than the current civilization, built with an instinct for threshold geometry that the current world has no framework to understand. Offering niches face inward rather than outward. Doorways are taller than they need to be. The carvings on the walls depict figures that are unmistakably humanoid but wrong in scale — giants, but not monstrous. Just large, the way they were.

They understood the boundary because they predated it. Their knowledge of the spirit world was not earned through practice or faith — it was their nature. The Audience Mantra carved into the deepest chamber wall is a remnant of that knowledge: a formal address to a being of cosmological rank, written in a language that is no longer spoken, by people who did not need the address because they could simply walk into Barong's domain directly.

When the split came, they did not survive it. The threshold temple, caught in the seam, preserved what they left inside it.

---

## The Remnants

The guards of Pura Adi are not alive in the way the living are alive. They are not undead in the way the corrupted are undead. They are remnants — echoes of the civilization's soldiers, held in place by the same duty that held their commander, present in the threshold because the threshold preserved everything that had been inside it when the split occurred.

They are large. Not Rakshasa-scaled — that is spirit world bulk, animal force. These are the proportions of their people: tall, broad, human in shape and bearing, simply built at a scale the current world does not produce. Their armor and weapons are of the old civilization's craft — stone and something that is not quite metal, worked in ways the current world does not know how to replicate.

They speak. The language is their civilization's — ancient in a way that makes Old Balinese sound recent, a tongue that died with the split and left no descendants and no records outside these walls. To any living person it would be sound without meaning: the formal challenge of guards at a post, words spoken to every intruder for millennia, heard by no one who understood them.

Natha understands them.

Barong is old enough that the language is not dead to him. The mask carries what Barong carries, which is older than the current religion and older than the split and older than the concept of a kingdom on this island. Natha hears the challenge and knows what it means without knowing how he knows. The words land with meaning the way words do when they have always been yours and you simply had not encountered them yet.

He is the first person to understand them in millennia. The remnants register this. They do not stand down — the duty is the duty — but something shifts in how they hold their positions. They have been speaking into silence for a very long time. The silence just answered.

When the duty ends, they do not fall. They simply are no longer present. The threshold had been holding them here. When it releases them, they return to wherever their people went.

---

## BOSS: The Guardian

The last of his civilization. Not a commander, not a specialist assigned to this post — one of the builders. His people constructed this temple in the world before the split, and when the split came and his civilization ended and everything else was gone, he stayed. Not because he chose to stay. Because the duty he was given had not been completed, and his people did not come to relieve him, and no one ever came to tell him it was over.

**Appearance**

Unmistakably humanoid. Enormous — the proportions of his civilization, which built the doorways here at their own scale. Not animalistic, not monstrous. Just large, and old, and still. His face is human: heavy-featured, the way faces become when they have held the same expression for long enough that it stops being an expression and becomes the face. His eyes register threat and nothing else. The emotional range that millennia of solitude might have done to a living person — grief, confusion, loneliness — is not visible. Whether it is absent or simply buried past any accessible depth is not answerable.

His armor is his civilization's craft. Heavier than it looks. It fits him the way old armor fits someone who has never taken it off.

The stone at his post is worn smooth where his feet have been. The wear is not from years. It is from long enough that the distinction between stone and time becomes unclear.

**The Challenge**

The Guardian speaks when Natha enters the inner chamber. He has spoken to every intruder who reached this point since his civilization fell. None of them understood. He spoke anyway, because the duty requires the challenge regardless of whether the challenged can hear it.

Natha hears it.

*"You wear my master's face. Let us see if you are worthy of it, young man."*

His master is Barong — or the being that became Barong, in the world before the split. This civilization served him. That is why the Audience Mantra is here: who else would preserve the formal address to the king of the spirit world except the people who served him directly.

He calls Natha young man. From where he stands, Natha is cosmically young. The entire current civilization is young. The word carries no condescension — just the honest perspective of something that has existed long enough that everyone it will ever meet is young by comparison.

He does not wait for an answer. The challenge was never a question. It is an announcement of what comes next.

**The Fight**

He does not fight with aggression. He fights with the specific force of something that has been doing one thing for so long the doing is all that remains. He is not trying to win. He is holding a position. The difference is legible in how he moves — no tactics, no adaptation. Just the door, and the thing standing between Natha and the door.

His scale makes him slow by the standards of the bosses Natha has already fought. He is not slow by any other standard.

The remnant guards in the outer chambers filter through during the fight — not coordinated with him, just responding to the same intrusion he is responding to. They do not require instruction. They know what they were left here to do.

**When He Falls**

He does not die. The fight ends — Natha lands whatever lands last — and the Guardian simply stops. Something leaves him. Whatever the duty was, it releases.

He does not fall. He fades. Between one moment and the next he is present and then he is not, and the absence is clean and complete and immediate, the way a sound ends. The remnant guards in the outer chambers go at the same time — simultaneously, without reaction, there and then not there.

The threshold temple is quiet. Possibly for the first time since the split. The worn stone at his post is still warm where his feet were.

He did not know his civilization was gone. He did not know how long it had been. He was not waiting to be relieved — he simply had not been relieved yet, and until he was, he would keep doing what he was told. He kept doing what he was told. The thing he was told to do is now done.

Whether that is tragedy or resolution is a question the threshold does not answer.

## The Audience Mantra

Carved into the wall of the innermost chamber. The old language — not translatable by Natha, not translatable by Wikananda, not fully translatable by anyone living. But the recitation is phonetic: the sounds are preserved, the correct pronunciation is embedded in the carving's rhythm, the ritual intent is communicable even across the death of the language that formed it.

This is the key to Communion Island. The key to Barong's palace. The key to the Dominated path.

It was not hidden here to be found eventually. It was left here because the civilization that wrote it understood what it was for, and built the place that contained it to be impassable to anyone who had not already earned the right to stand in front of it. The Guardian was not keeping the Audience Mantra from Natha. He was ensuring that whoever reached it had already proven they were not someone who should not have it.

Natha reads it. The language is not foreign to him — Barong carries it, and Natha carries Barong. He is the first person to read this with comprehension since the civilization that wrote it ended. He understands what he is saying. He understands what he is asking, who he is addressing, and what the formal address means in its full cosmological weight.

The boundary recognizes the mantra because it is correctly spoken, correctly understood, correctly intended. That is what makes it work.

## Notes

- Pura Adi is only relevant on the **Dominated path** — the Audience Mantra leads to Communion Island leads to Barong's palace. Players on Consumed or Sealed routes will find the hidden gate but have no reason to know what it opens, and may never open it.
- The civilization has no established name and should not be given one. They are older than names that survived. What they called themselves is not information that remains anywhere.
- The Guardian is the only one of his people still present in any form. When he disappears, his civilization is finally, completely gone. This does not require acknowledgment in the text. It is simply true.
- Pura Adi's architecture is the oldest thing in the game. Older than Rangda. Older than Barong's current form of worship. Older than the kingdom of Devata. The civilization that built it served Barong directly — the being that became Barong, in the world before the split. The Audience Mantra being preserved here is not coincidence. They wrote it. Of course his servants kept the formal address to their master. The game does not state this outright. The Guardian's words — *"you wear my master's face"* — make it available to anyone paying attention.
